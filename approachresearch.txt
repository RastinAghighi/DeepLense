Executive Summary
The research investigates the optimal configuration of the Enhanced Deep Super-Resolution (EDSR) network for upscaling a constrained dataset of 4,492 single-channel gravitational lensing images from 75x75 to 150x150 pixels. It concludes that a 16-residual-block EDSR architecture (~1.5 million parameters) is the definitive optimal choice, perfectly balancing representational capacity with computational efficiency while avoiding severe underfitting or overfitting.

Theoretical Scaling and Dataset Adequacy
Patch-Based Multiplier: While 4,492 image pairs may seem small, super-resolution models train on randomly extracted, overlapping spatial patches (e.g., 32x32). This effectively expands the training volume to hundreds of thousands of unique samples.

Empirical Thresholds: Cross-disciplinary research shows residual networks in localized regression tasks converge optimally with as few as 1,000–6,000 images. Furthermore, foundational models like EDSR were trained to state-of-the-art levels on merely 800 images (the DIV2K dataset). Thus, 4,492 images provide a statistically massive and robust training corpus.

Architectural Mechanics of EDSR
The EDSR framework relies on three critical optimizations for low-level image processing:

Removal of Batch Normalization: Crucial for super-resolution. Batch normalization discards absolute scale and brightness, destabilizing the network during small mini-batch training. Its removal preserves radiometric integrity and saves 40% in GPU memory.

Residual Scaling: Applies a static 0.1 scaling factor to residual block outputs, which stabilizes gradients and prevents numerical instability in deep/wide networks.

Sub-Pixel Convolution (PixelShuffle): Feature extraction occurs in the original low-resolution space. Upsampling only happens at the network's tail, drastically reducing computational latency.

Network Depth Evaluation (8 vs. 16 vs. 32 Blocks)
8 Blocks (SEDSR/EDSR-8): At ~0.6 million parameters, this configuration severely underfits. It lacks the spatial receptive field required to extract complex, hierarchical features, resulting in over-smoothed edges that fail downstream object detection algorithms.

32 Blocks (EDSR Standard): At ~43.0 million parameters, this model introduces a high risk of variance-limited scaling (memorizing specific sensor noise). It incurs immense computational costs for highly diminishing perceptual returns on domain-specific data.

16 Blocks (EDSR Baseline): The optimal configuration. At ~1.5 million parameters, it provides abundant topological depth to map complex non-linear structures without pushing the network into an unstable, over-parameterized state.

Morphological Divergence: Astronomical vs. Natural Images
Signal Sparsity: Unlike natural RGB images (which are dense, chaotic, and require massive parameter counts to memorize textures), astronomical images are highly sparse. They consist of dark backgrounds and predictable, continuous geometric gradients (Sérsic profiles, Einstein rings). Massive models are fundamentally unnecessary for this simplicity.

Flux Conservation: Scientific imaging strictly requires total integrated light (flux) to be conserved to maintain physical integrity. Over-parameterized models or Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) frequently hallucinate unphysical artifacts in pursuit of sharpness, ruining subsequent dark matter calculations.

Optimization and Regularization Protocols
To maximize the 16-block model's performance on the 4,492-image dataset, specific training protocols must be utilized:

Loss Formulation: L1 loss (Mean Absolute Error) is strictly preferred over L2 loss. L1 captures sharper geometric edges and stabilizes gradient convergence, whereas L2 tends to over-smooth textures.

The Core Problem
Astronomical deep learning (like Single-Image Super-Resolution or SISR) aims to enhance low-resolution telescope images. However, a fundamental conflict exists between the continuous, unbounded physical flux of astronomical data and the strictly bounded data requirements (e.g., [0,1] or [−1,1]) of traditional computer vision neural networks. This results in the frequent appearance of out-of-range pixel values (e.g., <0 or >1), forcing researchers to choose between algorithmic stability and photometric accuracy.

1. The Physical Origins of Out-of-Range Pixels
Out-of-range values in normalized astronomical arrays are not errors; they represent vital physical and statistical realities.

Negative Values (e.g., -0.0044): These are the unavoidable mathematical result of standard data reduction (removing bias, dark current, and sky background). Subtracting the mean sky brightness leaves a zero-mean noise distribution. Pixels fluctuating below the mean naturally become negative.

Danger of Clipping: Clamping these to 0.0 destroys the zero-mean property, artificially adding positive flux to the background and corrupting faint object measurements.

Super-Unity Values (e.g., 1.0108): Astrophysical phenomena have an unbounded dynamic range (e.g., faint galaxies vs. extremely bright quasars). To avoid compressing faint signals to zero, normalization often uses percentile clipping (e.g., the 99th percentile maps to 1.0). The top 1% of extremely bright pixels naturally scale above 1.0. Spatial interpolation (like Lanczos resampling) can also create mathematical overshoots >1.0.

Danger of Clipping: Clamping these to exactly 1.0 artificially flattens the central peaks of bright sources, destroying Point Spread Function (PSF) modeling.

2. The Deep Learning Dilemma: Clip vs. Preserve
Researchers must navigate a strict trade-off when feeding this data into neural networks.

The Argument for Clipping (Algorithmic Stability): Unbounded values cause exploding gradients and erratic loss convergence. Furthermore, networks with Sigmoid or Tanh final activation layers are mathematically restricted to (0,1) or (−1,1). If the ground truth contains a value of 1.0108, the network can never reach it, creating a permanent, unresolvable loss penalty.

The Argument for Preserving (Photometric Integrity): Preserving raw values allows the network to learn the true noise distribution. Clipping forces the network to hallucinate background flux or destroy the cores of bright Active Galactic Nuclei (AGN). Modern customized architectures specifically utilize physical priors and unclipped data to achieve "photometry-preserving" transformations.

3. Case Study: DiffLense and "Intensity Misalignment"
Conditional Diffusion Models (CDMs) like DiffLense require strict normalization bounds to function.

Mechanism: Diffusion models rely on a zero-centered Gaussian noise schedule. To prevent catastrophic error compounding during the reverse generative process, DiffLense iteratively clamps predictions to [−1,1] at every single step.

The Trade-off: While this yields unparalleled structural resolution (high PSNR), it induces Intensity Misalignment. The iterative truncation permanently breaks the linear photometric relationship, altering the absolute physical flux and resulting in lower SSIM scores.
